The science

What is collagen and why does it decline?

Collagen is a structural protein that makes up roughly 30% of all the protein in your body. It provides the framework that keeps your skin firm, your joints cushioned, your gut lining intact, and your bones dense.

From our mid-twenties, the body produces approximately 1% less collagen each year. Sun exposure, processed foods, stress, and poor sleep accelerate this decline — making targeted nutrition and lifestyle choices essential.

The good news: collagen synthesis can be actively supported. Vitamin C, zinc, glycine-rich foods, and reducing inflammation all stimulate the body's natural collagen production.

Why it matters

Four pillars of collagen health

✨

Skin firmness & glow

Collagen fibres give skin its structure and bounce. As levels decline, skin loses density and elasticity — leading to fine lines, sagging, and reduced radiance. Supporting collagen is the most direct route to youthful, resilient skin.

🦴

Joint & bone support

Collagen is the main component of cartilage — the cushioning between your joints. Rebuilding collagen supports joint comfort, reduces stiffness, and protects bone mineral density as you age.

🌿

Gut lining integrity

The gut lining is largely made of collagen. When it's compromised, undigested particles enter the bloodstream — triggering inflammation and food sensitivities. Collagen directly supports gut barrier repair.

💫

Hair, nails & energy

Collagen provides the amino acids needed for keratin synthesis — the building block of hair and nails. It also supports muscle recovery and metabolic function, influencing how energised you feel daily.
